Black-Scholes & The Greeks

A comprehensive guide to the mathematical framework underlying our options pricing engine. Understand the mechanics of risk management.

1. Introduction to Black-Scholes

The Black-Scholes model, also known as Black-Scholes-Merton, is a mathematical model for the dynamics of a financial market containing derivative investment instruments. From the partial differential equation in the model, known as the Black-Scholes equation, one can deduce the Black-Scholes formula, which gives a theoretical estimate of the price of European-style options.

Published in 1973 by Fischer Black and Myron Scholes, and later expanded by Robert Merton, this model revolutionized the options market and is widely used by options market participants today.

2. Assumptions of the Model

  • check_circle

    Log-normal Distribution

    The price of the underlying asset follows a geometric Brownian motion with constant drift and volatility.

  • check_circle

    No Arbitrage

    There are no riskless arbitrage opportunities.

  • check_circle

    Constant Risk-Free Rate

    The risk-free interest rate is constant and known.

  • check_circle

    European Options

    The options can only be exercised at the time of expiration.

3. The Pricing Formula

The value of a call option C or put option P is calculated as:

Call Option Price

C = S₀⋅N(d₁) - K⋅e⁻ʳᵀ⋅N(d₂)

Put Option Price

P = K⋅e⁻ʳᵀ⋅N(-d₂) - S₀⋅N(-d₁)

Where d₁ and d₂ are defined as:

d₁ = [ln(S₀/K) + (r + σ²/2)T] / (σ√T)

d₂ = d₁ - σ√T

4. Calculating the Greeks

Δ

Delta

Delta measures the rate of change of the theoretical option value with respect to changes in the underlying asset's price. It is the first derivative of the value V of the option with respect to the underlying price S.

// Call Delta
Δ_call = N(d₁)
// Put Delta
Δ_put = N(d₁) - 1
Γ

Gamma

Gamma measures the rate of change in the delta with respect to changes in the underlying price. Gamma is the second derivative of the value function with respect to the underlying price.

// Gamma (Same for Call & Put)
Γ = N'(d₁) / (S₀σ√T)
ν

Vega

Vega measures sensitivity to volatility. Vega is the derivative of the option value with respect to the volatility of the underlying asset.

// Vega (Same for Call & Put)
ν = S₀ ⋅ N'(d₁) ⋅ √T
Θ

Theta

Theta measures the sensitivity of the value of the derivative to the passage of time (see Option time value).

// Call Theta
Θ_call = -(S₀N'(d₁)σ) / (2√T) - rKe⁻ʳᵀN(d₂)
// Put Theta
Θ_put = -(S₀N'(d₁)σ) / (2√T) + rKe⁻ʳᵀN(-d₂)
ρ

Rho

Rho measures sensitivity to the interest rate. Rho is the derivative of the option value with respect to the risk free interest rate.

// Call Rho
ρ_call = K ⋅ T ⋅ e⁻ʳᵀ ⋅ N(d₂)
// Put Rho
ρ_put = -K ⋅ T ⋅ e⁻ʳᵀ ⋅ N(-d₂)
