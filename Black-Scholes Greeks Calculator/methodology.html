Black-Scholes & The Greeks

A comprehensive guide to the mathematical framework behind this options pricing calculator.

1. Introduction to Black-Scholes

The Black-Scholes model, also known as Black-Scholes-Merton, is a mathematical model for the dynamics of a financial market containing derivative investment instruments. From the partial differential equation in the model, one can deduce the Black-Scholes formula, which gives a theoretical estimate of the price of European-style options.

Published in 1973 by Fischer Black and Myron Scholes, and later expanded by Robert Merton, this model revolutionized the options market. Scholes and Merton received the 1997 Nobel Prize in Economics for this work (Black had passed away in 1995).

2. Assumptions of the Model

  • check_circle

    Log-normal Distribution

    The price of the underlying asset follows a geometric Brownian motion with constant drift and volatility.

  • check_circle

    No Arbitrage

    There are no riskless arbitrage opportunities in the market.

  • check_circle

    Constant Risk-Free Rate

    The risk-free interest rate is constant and known throughout the option's life.

  • check_circle

    European Options

    The options can only be exercised at the time of expiration (not early exercise).

  • check_circle

    No Transaction Costs

    There are no transaction costs or taxes associated with trading.

3. The Pricing Formula

The value of a call option C or put option P is calculated as:

Call Option Price

C = S·e−qT·N(d₁) − K·e−rT·N(d₂)

Put Option Price

P = K·e−rT·N(−d₂) − S·e−qT·N(−d₁)

Where d₁ and d₂ are defined as:

d₁ = [ln(S/K) + (r − q + σ²/2)T] / (σ√T)

d₂ = d₁ − σ√T

Variable Definitions

S Current stock price
K Strike price
T Time to expiration (years)
σ Volatility (annualized)
r Risk-free interest rate
q Continuous dividend yield
N(x) Standard normal CDF
N'(x) Standard normal PDF

4. Calculating the Greeks

The "Greeks" are partial derivatives of the option price with respect to various parameters. They measure different dimensions of risk in an options position.

Δ

Delta

Delta measures the rate of change of the option value with respect to changes in the underlying asset's price. It represents the equivalent position in shares.

// Call Delta (with dividend yield)
Δ_call = e−qT · N(d₁)
// Put Delta
Δ_put = e−qT · (N(d₁) − 1)
Γ

Gamma

Gamma measures the rate of change of Delta with respect to the underlying price. It's the second derivative of option value with respect to price, measuring convexity.

// Gamma (Same for Call & Put)
Γ = e−qT · N'(d₁) / (S · σ · √T)
ν

Vega

Vega measures sensitivity to volatility. It represents the change in option value for a 1% change in implied volatility.

// Vega (Same for Call & Put)
ν = S · e−qT · N'(d₁) · √T
Θ

Theta

Theta measures time decay — how the option's value decreases as time passes, assuming all else remains constant. Typically negative for long options.

// Call Theta (annualized)
Θ_call = −(S·e−qT·N'(d₁)·σ)/(2√T) − r·K·e−rT·N(d₂) + q·S·e−qT·N(d₁)
// Put Theta
Θ_put = −(S·e−qT·N'(d₁)·σ)/(2√T) + r·K·e−rT·N(−d₂) − q·S·e−qT·N(−d₁)
ρ

Rho

Rho measures sensitivity to interest rate changes. It represents the change in option value for a 1% change in the risk-free rate.

// Call Rho
ρ_call = K · T · e−rT · N(d₂)
// Put Rho
ρ_put = −K · T · e−rT · N(−d₂)
